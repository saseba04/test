Computer versus longhand note taking: Influence of revision
Abraham E. Flanigan1

· Kenneth A. Kiewra2 · Junrong Lu3 · Dzhovid Dzhuraev3

Received: 23 February 2021 / Accepted: 13 October 2022
© The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer Nature B.V. 2022

Abstract
Many college students believe that typing lecture notes on computers produces better notes
and higher achievement than handwritten lecture notes on paper. The few studies investigating computer versus longhand note taking yielded mixed note-taking and achievement findings. The present study investigated computer versus longhand note taking but
permitted note takers to revise or recopy notes during pauses interspersed throughout the
lecture. Moreover, the present study analyzed notes recorded while a lecture was ongoing and following revision pauses to determine if lecture ideas and images were recorded
completely or partially. Findings did not support the belief that computers aid note taking
and achievement and, instead, favored longhand note taking and revision. Computer and
longhand note takers recorded a comparable number of complete and partial ideas in notes
while the lecture was ongoing, but longhand note takers recorded more lecture images.
Among note revisers, longhand note takers added three-times-as-many complete ideas to
their notes during revision as computer note takers—an important finding because note
completeness predicted achievement. Achievement results showed that longhand note takers who revised notes scored more than half a letter grade higher on a lecture posttest than
computer note takers who revised notes. Present findings suggest that college instructors
should provide students with revision pauses to improve note taking and achievement and
encourage students to record and revise notes using the longhand method. Finally, regarding the computer versus longhand note-taking debate, the need to investigate further the
interplay between note-taking medium and lesson material is discussed.

Although most college students never receive formal note-taking training (Morehead et al.,
2019a), most take and review lecture notes as their primary test preparation strategy (Blasiman et al., 2017; Morehead et al., 2019a; Witherby & Tauber, 2019). Students are wise to
do so, because the number of lecture ideas recorded in notes (e.g., Bligh, 2000; Kiewra
et al., 1991a) and reviewed (e.g., Bui et al., 2012; Kiewra et al., 1991a; Knight & McKelvie, 1986) is positively associated with lecture test performance (Bui et al., 2012; Flanigan
& Titsworth, 2020; Morehead et al., 2019a). Moreover, when students are tested, many

students often fail to record many of the ideas and images conveyed during lectures (Bui
et al., 2012; Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020), leading researchers to examine factors impacting lecture note taking and subsequent achievement. Some contributing factors recently
examined include note-taking medium—whether notes are recorded using longhand or
computer mediums (e.g., Bui et al., 2012; Luo et al., 2018; Morehead et al., 2019a; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014), note completeness—whether notes are recorded completely or
partially (Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Peverly et al., 2007; Reddington et al., 2015), and
note revision—the opportunity to add or complete noted ideas during or following the lecture (Luo et al., 2016). The present study is the first to address all three of these factors
concomitantly. In doing so, the present study provides insight into whether incorporating
revision pauses during lectures—a practical strategy that can be easily implemented within
college classrooms or when students learn from recorded lectures—differentially affects
note completeness and achievement for students who type or hand write lecture notes.

Note‑taking medium
College students have traditionally taken lecture notes using a longhand (i.e., paper–pencil)
medium. However, the rise of mobile technology has led many students to instead type
notes on computers (Morehead et al., 2019b; Ragan et al., 2014; Ravizza et al., 2017).
Most students who type notes believe that doing so makes it easier to take notes during
class (Kay & Lauricella, 2011; Morehead et al., 2019b). As shown in Table 1, research
supports the notion that computers make it easier to note lesson ideas. Most students type
faster than they write (Brown, 1988), and those who type tend to record about 35% more
words (Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014) and 20% more ideas (Bui et al., 2012; Flanigan &
Titsworth, 2020) in their notes than those who write (Bui et al., 2012; Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Luo et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014).
Despite the speed advantage of typing, computer note takers do not consistently outperform longhand note takers on lecture learning assessments. As summarized in Table 1,
studies comparing note-taking mediums have produced mixed findings for a variety of
learning outcomes. When notes were recorded but not reviewed, one study found that students who took handwritten notes outperformed those who typed their notes on application
items (Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014). Another study showed that students who typed their
notes earned higher scores on a posttest taken immediately following a video lesson (Bui
et al., 2012). Yet, other studies favored neither medium on lecture posttest performance
(Luo et al., 2018; Morehead et al., 2019a). Similar inconsistencies emerged when notes
were reviewed before testing. Some studies showed that longhand note takers prevailed on
test items assessing factual and conceptual understanding (Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014)
and on items comprising total score (Luo et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014).
Other research showed that computer note takers prevailed on total posttest score (Fiorella
& Mayer, 2017). And some studies found that neither medium prevailed on items assessing
factual and conceptual understanding (Morehead et al., 2019a) nor on total posttest score
(Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Morehead et al., 2019a). Such inconsistent findings conflict
with the widely held student belief that typing lecture notes on computers improves learning beyond longhand note taking (Houle et al., 2013; Morehead et al., 2019b).

Researchers have identified three potential detriments to computer note taking. First,
despite the speed advantage of typing notes, computer note takers still record fewer than
half of the idea units presented during lectures (Bui et al., 2012; Flanigan & Titsworth,
2020; Morehead et al., 2019b). Second, many computer note takers use their speed advantage to transcribe lecture ideas in a verbatim fashion (Luo et al., 2018; Morehead et al.,
2019a; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014), indicative of shallow, non-meaningful processing. Longhand notetakers, meanwhile, record more paraphrased notes (Luo et al., 2018;
Morehead et al., 2019a; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014), indicative of deep, meaningful
processing (Craik & Lockhart, 1972). Longhand note taking has long been associated with
generative processing (e.g., Di Vesta & Gray, 1972; Luo et al., 2016), the meaningful integration of lesson ideas with previous knowledge (Wittrock, 1974). Third, the functionality
of a computer’s word processing programs makes it difficult for those who type notes to
record lecture-relevant images such as charts, graphs, and illustrations (Mosleh et al., 2016;
Reimer et al., 2009). In fact, those who type notes sometimes record no images during lectures (Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Luo et al., 2018).
In summary, although computers make it easier to record lecture ideas than hand-writing notes, typing notes has apparent downsides. Moreover, the inconsistent achievement
differences between computer and longhand note takers led researchers to conclude that
it is too early to declare one note-taking medium superior to the other (Morehead et al.,
2019a).

Note completeness
Most note-completeness investigations focus on the total number of lecture idea units
recorded in notes and their impact on achievement (Bui et al., 2012; Kiewra et al., 1991a;
Kiewra, 1985a, 1985b; Titsworth & Kiewra, 2004). According to Blohm and Colwell
(1983), an idea unit is a propositional statement that communicates meaning (e.g., causeeffect relationships, compare-contrast statements, examples, and independent facts). When
scoring lesson notes, credit is usually given for recording the full essence of the targeted
propositional statement. For instance, Morehead et al. (2019b) had college students
take notes during a video lecture and scored participants notes this way: “Each idea unit
included in the notes that was correct based on information in the video was given one
point” (p. 11). A flaw inherent in this and other note-scoring systems is their binary nature:
either a lesson idea is noted or it is not, despite its relative completeness.
Kiewra and colleagues have dug deeper into the note completeness issue by investigating what type or level of note is recorded, resulting in a more qualitative evaluation of
notes. In one study, Kiewra and Benton (1988) found that college students recorded 63% of
a lesson’s main ideas but only 33% of all information presented. This occurred because students failed to record many supporting ideas and details. In a related study (Kiewra et al.,
1987), lesson information was classified into level 1–4 information, with level 1 ideas
being most general and superordinate and levels 2–4 being progressively more detailed
and subordinate. College students recorded 37% of all lesson ideas but progressively fewer
ideas by level: level 1, 91%; level 2, 60%; level 3, 35%; and level 4, 11%. The conclusion
was that the quality of students’ notes is good where main ideas are concerned but weak
where subordinate details are concerned.
Some researchers have also recognized that students often record partial ideas and
images in notes and have begun scoring noted ideas and images as complete or partial

(Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Peverly et al., 2007; Reddington et al., 2015). For instance,
Flanigan and Titsworth (2020) had college students view a video lesson on the theory of
plate tectonics containing 143 complete ideas. The researchers reported that the statement,
“The earth’s crust is 25 miles thick underneath the continental surface,” represented one
complete idea unit that fully communicated the crust’s depth underneath the continental
surface. However, some note takers only captured part of that idea unit, such as “Crust = 25
miles,” omitting the rest of the idea— “underneath the continental surface.” Because the
depth of the crust differs underneath the continental (i.e., 25 miles thick) and seafloor (i.e.,
3–5 miles) surfaces, a student later reviewing this partially noted idea might be unable to
recall the surface to which this depth refers. In such cases, students’ notes were scored as
partially complete.
Based on McDougall’s (1904) threshold hypothesis, Flanigan and Titsworth (2020)
explored whether partially noted ideas would fall below the threshold needed to help students recall the complete idea unit from memory while later reviewing notes. Note-taking
results indicated that students, on average and regardless of note-taking medium, recorded
just 35% of lesson ideas, and that 20% of those noted ideas were partial notes. However, the
number of partially noted ideas was neither positively nor negatively predictive of achievement—as measured by total posttest score following the video lesson—nor was note-taking
medium. Rather, only the number of complete ideas recorded in notes predicted posttest
performance. Thus, Flanigan and Titsworth contended that finding ways to make partial
notes more complete could improve achievement. They proposed that providing students
with opportunities to add missed ideas to notes and to finish partially recorded ideas to
increase the number of complete ideas recorded could improve note taking and achievement. Such an opportunity is available when students revise the original lecture notes they
record.

Revision
Providing revision pauses during or following lectures improves note taking and achievement (Luo et al., 2016). Revision occurs between note taking and review and involves
learners making changes to their notes, such as when they add missing ideas or elaborate upon noted ideas. Luo and associates (2016) conducted two experiments to investigate how revision affects longhand note taking and achievement. In Experiment 1, students
either revised or recopied their lecture notes following an audio lesson. Recopying notes
is a common learning strategy used by college students to review notes (Jairam & Kiewra, 2010; Karpicke et al., 2009). Those who revised their notes recorded more lesson
ideas and learned more relationships than did those who recopied their notes. Experiment
2 revealed that revising notes multiple times throughout the lesson led to more complete
notes and higher achievement than did revising notes just once after the lesson ended, with
revision time equal for all. Moreover, it was evident that students completed some partial
ideas during revision by adding missed information to noted ideas—a process referred to
as extension. The researchers, however, did not assess the number of extensions revisers
made during revision nor their impact on achievement. Moreover, Luo and associates only
investigated how revision affected longhand note taking. It remains unknown how computer note takers might benefit from revision opportunities and whether revision differentially affects note completeness and achievement across note-taking mediums.

The revision benefits uncovered by Luo and associates (2016) led them to propose that
note taking is best conceptualized as a three-stage process: recording, revising, and reviewing. To date, all known investigations comparing computer and longhand note-taking mediums have conceptualized note taking as a one-stage recording process (Bui et al., 2012,
Experiments 1 and 2; Morehead et al., 2019a, Experiments 1 and 2; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014, Experiments 1 and 2) or two-stage recording and reviewing process (Bui et al.,
2012, Experiment 3; Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Luo et al., 2018; Morehead et al., 2019a,
Experiment 3; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014; Experiment 3). However, because students
take relatively incomplete notes while lectures are ongoing (Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020;
Luo et al., 2016; Titsworth, 2004), offering revision opportunities should help students
record and review more notes. The present study is the first to include all three note-taking
stages from Luo and associates’ (2016) three-stage framework when comparing outcomes
for computer and longhand note takers. Researchers have previously struggled to reach a
consensus regarding whether the computer or longhand medium is better for taking notes
(e.g., Morehead et al., 2019b). It is plausible that these mixed findings stem from prior
investigations including an incomplete accounting of the note-taking process.

Factors affecting retrieval during revision
Educational researchers have proposed that taking notes serves two potential functions for
learners: (a) encoding incoming information into memory as notes are recorded (i.e., the
encoding function) and (b) having an external record of lecture ideas that are reviewed and
committed to memory after the lecture ends (i.e., the external storage function) (e.g., Di
Vesta & Gray, 1972; Kiewra et al., 1991a, 1991b). Learners who take and review notes—
the external-storage function—typically score higher on achievement tests than learners
who take but do not review notes—the encoding function (e.g., Carter & Van Matre, 1975;
Luo et al., 2018; Robinson & Kiewra, 1995). The cognitive demands placed on learners
during lecture learning (e.g., continuously listening, identifying and interpreting incoming information, and recording notes) seems to leave few cognitive resources available
for the kind of generative processing needed to learn (Peters, 1972). Thus, the maximum
benefit of recording lecture notes seems to emerge after students review notes. Moreover,
the benefit of reviewing notes is naturally increased when notes are more complete (e.g.,
Kiewra, 1985a, 1985b; Luo et al., 2016). Revision is a means for increasing note completeness. Revision is an often-overlooked stage within the note-taking process that falls
between recording and reviewing notes (Luo et al., 2016). Although revision is an avenue
for increasing note quantity (Luo et al., 2016), its benefit depends on learners recalling
and adding missing lecture ideas to their notes when revising. As discussed by Luo et al.
(2016), lecture ideas recorded in learners’ notes can serve as retrieval cues (Thomson &
Tulving, 1970; Tulving & Osler, 1968) during revision to prompt recall of lecture ideas
that went unrecorded during the lecture. However, prior research suggests that two factors
might affect how well lecture notes serve as retrieval cues during revision: note completeness and note-taking medium.

Note completeness, retrieval, and revision
It is plausible that the completeness of the idea units recorded in students’ lecture notes
affects the utility of using those notes as retrieval cues. For instance, after examining the
presence of partial and complete idea units in student notes and their relationship with
learning, Flanigan and Titsworth (2020) postulated that partially recorded idea units might
serve as effective retrieval cues because they surpass McDougall’s (1904) recognition
threshold necessary to recall the complete idea unit. For instance, a student revising the
partially recorded idea, “Hanging wall up” might be cued to recall the complete idea, “The
hanging wall moves upward in a reverse fault.” If so, partial notes should provide ample
cues for recalling and noting the complete lecture idea during the revision process.
Complete ideas stored within student notes might also serve as effective retrieval cues
during revision, in this case helping students to recall and note other lecture ideas not originally recorded. Through the process of spreading activation (Anderson, 1990), reviewing complete ideas can activate recall of other related lecture ideas. Similarly, Tulving
and Patterson (1968) discussed the notion of functional units—conceptually-related idea
units that stimulate retrieval of one another from memory. For instance, the completely
recorded idea, “The hanging wall moves upward in a reverse fault,” could cue the recall of
the related but unnoted idea, “Reverse faults are caused by compressional stress.” In this
instance, the presence of one idea related to reverse faults within a student’s notes facilitates recall of another reverse fault idea during revision. Thus, complete idea units could
plausibly serve as effective retrieval cues by facilitating activation and recall of other functionally related ideas.
As discussed, both partially and completely recorded lecture ideas should facilitate
retrieval of originally non-noted lecture ideas during the revision process. If so, revision
could improve both the completeness of partially recorded notes and the recording of
related lecture ideas not originally recorded in notes. However, what remains unknown is
the extent to which revisers finish the partial ideas and images recorded in their notes. Do
revisers naturally try to complete the partial ideas and images contained in their notes? Do
they focus most of their revision efforts on adding missing ideas and images? To date, the
relationship between revision and note completeness remains understudied, making it difficult for researchers and practitioners to recommend how students can improve their revision strategies. Thus, an overarching goal of the present study is to address this literature
gap by embedding revision pauses into an ongoing lecture.

Note‑taking medium, retrieval, and revision
Note completeness is not the only factor that can affect revision. The theoretical assumptions of Craik and Lockhart’s (1972) depth of processing framework suggest that note-taking medium also affects the utility of using one’s lecture notes as retrieval cues. Craik and
Lockhart suggested that memory for learned information is a function of the depth to which
that information was processed. Mueller and Oppenheimer (2014) proposed that longhand
note taking engages learners in deeper processing of incoming information than typing lecture notes. The researchers contend that because longhand note takers cannot record lecture
ideas as quickly as students who type their notes, longhand note takers tend to be more
selective about which ideas they note and tend to paraphrase noted ideas more often, reflective of deep and meaningful processing (Luo et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014).
Computer users, meanwhile, tend to transcribe lecture ideas in a verbatim form, potentially

reflective of shallow processing (Luo et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014). Such
findings support the notion that longhand notes provide learners with a more meaningful
record of lecture ideas to review and commit to memory. When these findings are extrapolated to a revision session, it is plausible that the more meaningful notes generated by longhand note takers would make those notes more effective retrieval cues.

The present study
Many college students take relatively incomplete lecture notes regardless of their note-taking medium (Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Luo et al., 2018; Morehead et al., 2019b), which
is problematic considering students’ reliance on taking and reviewing notes as their primary test-preparation strategies (Blasiman et al., 2017; Witherby & Tauber, 2019) and considering the positive correlation between note completeness and achievement (Bui et al.,
2012; Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Morehead et al., 2019a). The purpose of the present
study was to investigate whether note completeness and achievement could be bolstered by
providing longhand and computer note takers with revision pauses throughout an ongoing
lecture. We predicted that revision would benefit longhand note takers more than computer
note takers. Researchers have suggested that the speed deficit experienced by longhand
note takers actually prompts them to be more deliberate, selective, and generative while
taking notes compared to computer note takers (Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014) who often
type verbatim notes mechanically with minimal meaningful processing (Luo et al., 2018;
Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014). Because of this more deliberate, thoughtful note-taking
process, we believed longhand notes would more easily surpass McDougall’s (1904) recognition threshold and act as better retrieval cues during revision than typed notes. Thus,
we predicted that longhand note takers would add more new ideas and images to their
notes during revision and finish more of the partial ideas and images they had recorded
previously than computer note takers. Furthermore, partially recorded ideas should serve
as effective retrieval cues during revision because they surpass the recognition threshold
(McDougall, 1904) needed to recall the complete idea. Thus, we predicted that most of
the complete idea units gained during revision pauses would occur through completion of
partial idea units.
The interplay among note-taking medium, revision, note completeness, and achievement remains unknown. All previous investigations comparing computer and longhand
note-taking mediums conceptualized note taking as either a one-step (record-only) or twostep (record and review) process. The present study is the first to add the intermediary
stage of revision and compare computer and longhand mediums across all three note-taking stages of the note-taking framework proposed by Luo and associates (2016). Addressing this literature gap should empower educators to make more well-informed decisions
about using revision pauses during their lessons, recommending whether students use computer or longhand mediums to take notes, and helping students improve their revision strategies to enhance note completeness.

Materials

Materials included an audio-visual lesson and note-taking materials.
The audio-visual lesson was on the topic of plate tectonics. The approximately 21-min
lesson contained 2,236 spoken words and was presented at a rate of 107 words per minute
by an adult male reading from a teleprompter script. The lesson also contained 17 images
vital to understanding the spoken lesson. Throughout the lesson, an image was shown most
of the time. Only four times did the lecturer appear instead, and only for about two minutes
in total. The lesson was divided into three fairly equal-length segments. The first segment
lasted 5 min and 30 s and included these sections: Introduction, The Earth’s Layers, and
Tectonic Plates (and three images). The second segment lasted 7 min and 30 s and included
these sections: Convergent Boundary, Divergent Boundary, and Transform Boundary (and
seven images). The third segment lasted 7 min and included these sections: Fault Blocks
and Types of Faults (and seven images). Appendix 1 shows an image from each lesson segment and the spoken words that accompanied each image.
Note-taking materials were paper- or computer-based. Longhand note takers were given
three sheets of lined paper, each marked for one of the three lecture segments, and a black
pen for note taking. Longhand note takers in the revision group were also given a red pen
for revising their notes so that lesson notes and revision notes could later be identified and
assessed. Computer note takers recorded notes in a Word document on a Desktop PC with
a 21-inch screen. The note-taking document had three labeled sections, one for each lecture
segment. Computer note takers in the revision group accessed the Track Changes function to
make revisions so that lesson notes and revision notes could later be identified and assessed.
Computer note takers were briefly trained to use Track Changes prior to the beginning of the
study. Participants in the no revision (copy notes) groups were given additional paper (longhand note takers) or access to an additional Word document screen (computer note takers)
to copy lesson notes. Note copying also served as the experimental control in the Luo et al.
study (2016). Note copying was used in both cases because it is a common student practice
following lectures (Jairam & Kiewra, 2010; Karpicke et al., 2009), and because it prevents
students from revising or studying notes during the revision pause.

Measures included a vocabulary task, test, and survey.
An 8-item vocabulary task was used as an interpolated task to clear working memory
between note review and testing. Here is a sample item:

The survey was used to collect demographic information about age, gender, race, year
in college, grade-point average, college major, typical note-taking medium (computer or

choice, open-ended, or Likert-like scale.

Procedure
On Day 1, participants in each of six different sections of the same educational psychology course, each meeting at a different time of day, met in their regular classrooms where
they were assigned randomly to one of the four experimental groups. Those assigned to
the longhand note-taking group remained in their regular classroom, and those assigned to
the computer note-taking group went to a nearby computer lab. The lab was a closed classroom with 20 desktop computers used only for the experiment.
Following introductory remarks about the experiment’s purpose (to investigate note taking) and about general and group-specific procedures, all participants viewed the tectonic
plates lesson, which was shown on a large screen in the front of each room, and recorded
notes as directed (longhand or computer) and “in a way that is most helpful to you.” Prior
to the beginning of the video lesson, participants were informed that they would review
their notes and be tested over the video lesson in two days.
At the end of each of the three lesson segments, there was a 3-min pause. Those in the

Data analysis
All lecture notes were scored for idea unit completeness using a scoring sheet that included
all 198 lecture ideas. A lecture idea unit, according to Blohm and Cowell (1983), is a single
proposition that communicates meaning (e.g., cause-effect relationships, comparisons, examples, or isolated facts). A sample idea unit in the present study was: “The hanging wall moves
downward in a normal dip-slip fault.” Participants received one point for each complete idea
unit contained in their notes. However, because students often record partial ideas in their
notes (e.g., Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Peverly et al., 2007; Reddington et al., 2015), we followed Flanigan and Titsworth’s (2020) procedure of scoring notes as being either partial (i.e.,
“concept-identification only” or “concept-identification + incomplete details”) or complete.
Table 4 provides an example of partial and complete ideas. As discussed by Flanigan and
Titsworth (2020), because the full meaning of one idea unit can often be derived from other
noted information (i.e., headings, signals), ideas were only scored as partial if their meaning
could not be derived from other information within the participant’s notes. Using these scoring mediums, revision notes were scored for the number of complete ideas added, partial ideas
finished, and partial ideas added.
All notes were also scored for the complete or partial recording of the 17 lecture images.
Images were scored as complete if they contained all discernable information contained in the
image that was presented during the video lesson—such as all layers and labels of the earth’s
lithosphere. Images were scored as partial if they lacked discernable information contained in
the image—such as missing layers or labels for the earth’s lithosphere. Revision notes were
scored for the number of complete images added, partial images finished, and partial images
added.
Two members of the research team independently scored one-fifth of participants’ notes to
establish reliability for the note-scoring procedures. Interrater agreement between the two note
scorers was 0.81 for ideas and 0.87 for images, both of which represent strong levels of agreement (Cohen, 1988). Disagreements were resolved by discussion. Each scorer then scored half
of the remaining notes independently. Raters also independently confirmed that participants in
the copy notes groups followed directions and did not edit their notes.
Posttest items were scored using an objective scoring sheet. Each item had one correct
answer based on the information presented during the video lesson. A total posttest score was
generated, and posttests were also scored by item type: factual, relationship, and concept. Participants received one point for each item answered correctly—for a total of 30 points.
All statistical analyses used the cutoff value of p < .05 as the significance threshold. Analysis of variance (ANOVA) and multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA) were used to
examine group differences across note-taking and achievement outcomes. Linear regression
models examined whether note-taking outcomes accounted for statistically detectable variance
in total posttest scores. Partial eta squared (ηp2) is reported as the effect size for ANOVA and
MANOVA analyses. As discussed by Cohen (1988) and Richardson (2011), observed ηp2 values of 0.01, 0.06, and 0.14 or higher are indicative of small, medium, and large effect sizes,
respectively. Adjusted ­R2 is reported as the proportion of variance in the outcome variables
accounted for by the regression models.

Results pertain to preliminary analyses, note-taking outcomes, and achievement. Notes
taken while the lesson was ongoing and during revision pauses were scored. Table 5 presents means and standard deviations across note-taking medium (computer or longhand)
and revision (revision or no revision), and Table 6 presents means and standard deviations

Preliminary analyses
Chi-square and ANOVA tests were conducted for demographic variables, prior knowledge,
and preferred note-taking medium. Appendix 2 provides group statistics for these analyses. There was a statistically detectable difference among the four note-taking groups on
the alignment between their preferred note-taking medium (typing or writing notes) and
the medium they were assigned in the present study (p = .012). Specifically, longhand note
takers experienced misalignment more than computer note takers. When the covariate and
the experimental effect are not independent, the treatment effect is obscured and spurious
treatment effects can arise and compromise analysis of covariance interpretation (Miller &
Chapman, 2001; Wildt & Ahtola, 1978). Thus, note-taking alignment was not treated as a
covariant and was instead included within subsequent analyses as an independent variable.
There were no statistically detectable differences among the four note-taking groups on any
other demographic, prior knowledge, or note-taking variables.

Discussion
The rapid emergence of mobile technology has led many college students to believe that
computers make it easier to take lecture notes (Kay & Lauricella, 2011; Witherby & Tauber, 2019) and to learn (Houle et al., 2013; Morehead et al., 2019a, 2019b). Previous
research yielded inconsistent note-taking and achievement differences between longhand
and computer note takers (e.g., Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Luo et al., 2018; Morehead
et al., 2019a, 2019b). However, those prior studies conceptualized note taking as either a
one-stage (i.e., recording-only) or two-stage (i.e., recording and reviewing) process. The
present study is the first to compare computer and longhand note-taking mediums across
three note-taking stages (i.e., recording, revising, and reviewing). Because note taking
might be best conceptualized according to this three-stage process (Luo et al., 2016), it
is plausible that inconsistencies across previous investigations stemmed from this incomplete framing of the note-taking process. Perhaps one note-taking medium is superior to
the other after accounting for all three note-taking stages. The present study addressed
this shortcoming and was the first to investigate systematically the main and interactive
effects of note-taking medium (computer, longhand) and revision (revision, no revision)
on college students’ note taking—complete and partial lesson ideas and images—and

13

A. E. Flanigan et al.

achievement. Next, findings are discussed with respect to note completeness prior to and
during revision and to posttest achievement.

Does note‑taking medium impact note completeness prior to revision?
Findings from the present study do not support the notion that computers improve
note-taking outcomes. Regardless of note-taking medium, participants failed to record
most of the ideas presented during the lesson. Across note-taking groups, participants
recorded about 30% of lesson ideas with about 20% of lesson ideas being completely
recorded and almost 10% of lesson ideas being partially recorded while the video lesson
was ongoing. The sum of complete and partial notes recorded was on par with previous
research (e.g., Bui et al., 2012; Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Luo et al., 2018; Morehead
et al., 2019a, 2019b) showing that students commonly record just one-third of lesson
ideas. In the present study, though, it is evident that a statistically detectable portion of
noted ideas are incomplete transcriptions.
That longhand and computer note takers recorded a similar number of ideas while
the lesson was ongoing is surprising. Previous studies found that students who type
their notes record more lecture ideas than students who write their notes (e.g., Bui et al.,
2012; Luo et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014)—presumably because most students can type faster than they can write (Brown, 1988). It is plausible that the intermittent pauses embedded within the video lesson helped longhand note takers stave off
fatigue and write more than if the lecture was continuous. Future research should further investigate how lecture pauses and other instructional aids differentially affect computer and longhand note taking.
As to why students recorded a high percentage of partial ideas in their notes, we propose
two possible explanations. First, students might feel rushed while taking notes and are unable to completely record all the ideas they intend to record in notes. Even though the rate of
speech for the present study’s lesson was a modest 107 words per minute (wpm) (whereas
most lecture rates are about 175 wpm, Simonds et al., 2006), most college students only
write about 20 wpm (Connelly et al., 2005) and type about 50 wpm (Dhakal et al., 2018),
making it likely that students felt rushed while taking notes. Second, as discussed by Flanigan and Titsworth (2020), students might intuitively believe that partially recorded idea
units overcome McDougall’s (1904) recognition threshold and aid recall of the complete
idea while later reviewing notes. Thus, students might intentionally record partial ideas
in their notes intending to use them as retrieval cues later for recalling and perhaps noting
missing information. Regardless of the explanation, research investigating the prevalence
of partial ideas in student notes is still in its infancy. Only one other known study (Flanigan
& Titsworth, 2020) indexed the number of partial ideas in student notes. Thus, it would
be useful for future research to explain the presence of partial ideas in lesson notes. Such
research could guide interventions designed to help students record more complete notes.
Longhand note takers did record more images in their notes while the lesson was
ongoing than computer note takers. Despite having at least one image displayed on the
screen during approximately 90% of the video lesson, only two computer users recorded
any lesson-related images in their notes—and these students only recorded two of the 17
images apiece. The images that computer users did record in notes were relatively simple
images—such as two arrows pointing toward each other to represent a convergent plate
boundary. The functionality of word processing programs likely made it difficult for computer users to note more complex images (Mosleh et al., 2016; Reimer et al., 2009). The

13

Computer versus longhand note taking: Influence of revision﻿	

observed effect size comparing the number of images recorded by computer and longhand
note takers while the lesson was ongoing was large (ηp2 = .361). However, this effect size
itself does not capture the practical finding that longhand note takers were not much better
at recording images in notes than computer users, as the former only fully recorded about
20% of the displayed images and partially recorded another 10% of the displayed images
while the lesson was ongoing.
Overall, it was evident that students recorded a small percentage of lecture ideas and
images in notes, regardless of note-taking medium. Although the absence of noted images
by computer note takers likely drove the large effect size uncovered for this analysis, present findings cannot explain why longhand note takers recorded so few images as well.
Findings do suggest that students, in general, place more emphasis on recording text-based
information than images in notes. Evidently, students need instruction in recognizing the
value of and recording lesson images.
Lesson note taking was affected when students knew they would intermittently revise
their notes. Revisers recorded about three more partial ideas while the lesson was ongoing
than participants who knew they would intermittently recopy their notes. A tentative explanation, mentioned earlier, is that revisers intentionally adapted their in-lecture note-taking
strategy so as to record partial ideas in notes to use as retrieval cues later while studying or
with the intention of finishing them during revision. More research is needed to determine
if this is a deliberate strategy or a spurious finding.
Overall, findings revealed many similarities in the notes taken by longhand and computer note takers while the lesson was ongoing. Both groups completely recorded about
20% of the ideas presented during the lesson, while partially capturing another 10% of
the lesson ideas. Students who type their notes tend to transcribe lecture ideas in a verbatim fashion, which has been associated with shallower information processing relative to
taking handwritten notes (Luo et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014), but present
findings indicate that both note-taking methods yield incomplete notes while lectures are
ongoing. Although longhand note takers recorded more images in their notes than computer note takers, many of those images were also partially recorded. Moreover, longhand
note takers only completely recorded about one-third of the images presented during the
lesson. Overall, it was evident that students recorded a small percentage of lecture ideas
and images in notes while the lesson was ongoing, regardless of note-taking medium. This
finding aligns with long-standing literature on the relative incompleteness of lecture notes
when students are not afforded the opportunity to revise notes during lessons (Austin et al.,
2004; Titsworth, 2004).
Note-taking alignment affected the number of partial ideas recorded in notes while
the lesson was ongoing. For the longhand note-taking groups, misalignment with preferred note-taking medium increased the number of partial ideas recorded in notes relative
to alignment. Specifically, participants who preferred to type their notes, but who were
assigned to one of the longhand note-taking groups, recorded more “concept-identification
only” partial ideas than participants assigned to one of the longhand note-taking groups
who preferred to take longhand notes. Yet, for the computer note-taking groups, alignment
with preferred note-taking medium increased the number of these partial ideas recorded
in notes relative to misalignment. Taken together, these findings suggest that students who
prefer to type their lecture notes also tend to record more partial ideas into their notes.
Such findings contradict the claim that computer users transcribe complete lecture ideas
verbatim (Luo et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014). However, none of these prior
studies scored for the number of partial ideas contained in notes, and the present study
did not assess verbatim transcription as did studies making this claim. Going forward, we

13

A. E. Flanigan et al.

recommend that investigations into computer versus longhand note-taking account for the
presence of partial ideas in participant notes to attain a more comprehensive view of the
notes taken by computer users.

Does note‑taking medium impact note completeness during revision?
Revision improved the quantity (i.e., number of ideas and images) and completeness of
lecture notes. Regarding lecture ideas, the revision group finished with about four more
complete ideas and four more partial ideas in their notes than the no-revision group. Revision group participants also recorded more partial ideas in their notes while the lesson
was ongoing than the no-revision group, perhaps knowing they would have an opportunity to add to their notes later. Longhand note takers particularly benefited from revision
pauses. Longhand note takers added three-times-as-many complete ideas (ηp2 = .143) and
two-times-as-many partial ideas (ηp2 = .073) to their notes during revision as computer
note takers. These note-taking outcomes and large effect sizes supported our prediction
that revision pauses would benefit longhand note takers more than computer note takers.
One possible explanation for this finding is depth of processing theory (Craik & Lockhart,
1972). It has been established that students who type their notes tend to transcribe lecture
ideas verbatim, which is representative of shallow processing; whereas longhand note takers tend to paraphrase lecture ideas, which is representative of deeper, more meaningful
processing (e.g., Luo et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014). It is plausible, then, that
deeper processing by longhand note takers during the lecture makes it easier for them, than
for computer notetakers, to use their notes as retrieval cues during revision. Future research
should investigate whether deeper processing by longhand note takers produces notes more
likely to surpass the recognition threshold (McDougall, 1904) than typed notes and better
prompt recall of other lecture ideas during revision. Such research could challenge or support the notion that typing notes leads to shallower information processing during lecture
learning than does handwriting notes.
Contrary to our prediction, revisers devoted most of their revision efforts to adding
ideas they missed entirely while the lesson was ongoing instead of finishing the partial
ideas contained in their notes. In total, revisers netted 368 complete ideas during revision,
and 60% of these were the addition of missed ideas, and 40% of these were partial ideas
that had been finished during revision. However, given that revisers were more likely than
non-revisers to record partial ideas while the lesson was ongoing—knowing they would
have time to revise their notes later—the tendency of revisers to finish partial ideas during
revision seems to support the notion that partial ideas act as retrieval cues for recalling and
perhaps noting the complete lesson idea. However, revisers only finished about 11% of the
partial ideas contained in their notes—leaving a considerable number of ideas unfinished.
Thus, although finishing partial ideas was a common strategy among revisers, many partial
ideas remained unfinished in their notes. Consequently, present and previous (Luo et al.,
2016) findings support revision as a viable strategy for helping college students record
more complete lecture notes, but notes will remain far from complete until note-taking and
revision methods are improved.
Findings from the present study and from prior research suggest that some of these
improvements can come from encouraging students to take longhand notes (e.g., Luo
et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014), identifying ways to help students record more
complete notes while lectures are ongoing (e.g., Luo et al., 2018; Morehead et al., 2019a),
allowing time for revision during or after lessons (e.g., Luo et al., 2016), and letting

13

Computer versus longhand note taking: Influence of revision﻿	

students compare notes with a partner to increase note quantity and completeness (Luo
et al., 2016). Such strategies have been shown to improve either note-taking quantity, completeness, or both.
It was surprising that most idea units gained during revision came by adding idea units
that had gone completely un-noted while the lesson was ongoing. Contrary to our prediction, it appears that revisers devoted most of their revision efforts toward recalling missing idea units and not toward finishing partially recorded idea units, even though partially
noted ideas should easily overcome the recognition threshold (McDougall, 1904) and aid
retrieval of complete idea units. Although tentative, present findings support Flanigan and
Titsworth’s (2020) proposition that students believe—either accurately or inaccurately—
that partial ideas serve as adequate retrieval cues while studying notes later and, therefore, need not be completed during revision. If this proposition is accurate, then it perhaps explains why participants committed most of their revision efforts toward recalling
and adding idea units that were missing from their notes rather than completing partial
idea units. Yet, when taken together, present and prior findings (Flanigan & Titsworth,
2020) uncovered no relationship—either positive or negative—between the number of partial ideas recorded in notes and posttest achievement. Thus, it remains too early to claim
whether partially recorded idea units surpass the recognition threshold (McDougall, 1904)
and whether recording and reviewing partial ideas hinders or facilitates lecture recall.
Given that the primary advantage comes in the review of notes than in their recording
(i.e., note taking’s external-storage function), the presence of partially recorded ideas and
images might undermine the utility of reviewing notes if partially recorded information
falls below the recognition threshold. As previously mentioned, future research should
determine why students record partial notes, why they leave the bulk of them unfinished,
and how partial notes impact lesson information retrieval during revision across note-taking mediums and, ultimately, achievement.
Regarding the noting of lecture-related images, longhand note taking was superior to
computer note taking. No computer users added or finished lecture-related images during
revision. Previous research showed that computer users struggle to record lecture-related
images while lessons are ongoing (Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Luo et al., 2018). The present study is the first to show that these struggles for computer users extend to revision
pauses. However, longhand note takers did not devote much effort toward improving the
number of images contained in their notes either. Although a large effect size (ηp2 = .174)
was observed for note-taking medium (Cohen, 1988), this large effect might be misleading because both longhand and computer note takers actually added few images during
revision. On average, longhand revisers added less than one image to their notes during
revision—and practically none of them finished partial images. These findings suggest that
revisers either (a) prioritized ideas over images or (b) had difficulty recalling and recording
images during revision. Hopefully, future research can uncover techniques that help students—particularly computer users—add or finish more images during revision in order to
improve the quantity and completeness of lecture notes.
Furthermore, future research should examine how lesson content affects note completeness and the benefit of revision pauses across different note-taking mediums. Present
and previous research included lesson content containing quite a bit of imagery—such as
the theory of plate tectonics in the present study and reinforcement schedules in previous
research (Luo et al., 2016). Because typing notes is usually faster than hand writing them
(Brown, 1988), it is plausible that revision benefits are lessened for computer note takers
when lesson content contains more images. Future research should continue to investigate

13

A. E. Flanigan et al.

the interplay among note-taking medium, revision pauses, and note completeness across
lessons that include varying amounts of imagery.

How do note‑taking medium and revision impact achievement?
Many students believe that typing notes makes it easier to learn than writing notes (e.g.,
Morehead et al., 2019b). However, previous research has not consistently supported this
belief, as shown in the summary of previous findings presented in Table 1. Present findings
also contradict this belief. Longhand note takers scored more than one-half of a letter grade
higher (ηp2 = .063) on the posttest than computer note takers—regardless of whether notes
were revised, with achievement advantages particularly resident for fact and concept learning. Although a medium-sized effect was observed (Cohen, 1988), the practical interpretation (i.e., computer users scored one-half letter grade lower on the posttest) seems most
relevant for educational researchers and practitioners. The lack of support for the popular
belief that computers make learning easier stemming from present and prior research (e.g.,
Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Luo et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014) suggests
that educators should counsel students about the research literature comparing longhand
and computer note-taking mediums and help dispel this myth. As previously suggested,
although it is plausible that lesson content is the best determinant for whether typing or
hand writing notes is most beneficial, present and previous findings suggest that typing
one’s lecture notes does not consistently improve learning like many college students
believe (Houle et al., 2013; Morehead et al., 2019a).
Posttest performance was predicted by the number of complete ideas contained in
student notes after all note-taking activities had finished. The number of complete ideas
recorded while the lesson was ongoing and added during revision accounted for about 15%
of the variance in posttest scores. Previous research also showed that giving students time
to revise their notes during a lesson improved note taking and achievement (Luo et al.,
2016). Based on previous and present findings, educators should provide lecture pauses
for note revision and advocate that students pause prerecorded lectures they watch on their
own in order to revise their notes (see also Kiewra et al., 1991b).
The number of partial ideas contained in student notes did not predict posttest achievement. However, this finding does not imply that partially noted ideas cannot be leveraged
to improve student learning. Because the present study and previous research (Flanigan &
Titsworth, 2020; Luo et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014) found a positive relationship between the number of complete ideas recorded and achievement, it seems plausible that training students to identify and finish partial ideas during revision could boost
achievement. Our participants finished only a small number of the partial ideas in their
notes. Failure to transform so many ideas from partial to complete could explain why the
revision group did not outperform the no-revision group on the posttest.

Limitations and future research
The present study had limitations that could be addressed in future research. The first pertains
to lecture content. The theory of plate tectonics topic was chosen because it was correctly
anticipated that most participants would report little prior knowledge about this topic. However, because the topic was unrelated to the educational psychology course from which participants were recruited, it is possible that some participants lacked motivation to learn about

13

Computer versus longhand note taking: Influence of revision﻿	

this topic. Future research could address this limitation by providing participants with lesson
topics relevant to the course(s) from which they were recruited or also by assessing student’s
motivation while completing the experiment. A second limitation was that desktop computers
were used to type notes rather than laptop computers as in previous research (e.g., Bui et al.,
2012; Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Luo et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014). It was
not feasible to provide participants with laptop computers, and we did not want them using
their own laptop in order to standardize note-taking functions. To ensure that all potential participants had the opportunity to participate, data collection was held in a university computer
laboratory where participants were provided with readily available desktop computers to take
notes. Future studies might issue standard laptops to all students or allow them to use their
own. A third limitation, perhaps, is that computer note takers revised notes using the tracked
changes function of the Word processing program. This function might have been unfamiliar
to some users or have produced a revised document that was difficult to read and process.
Future studies might explore other revision mediums for computer note taking. A fourth limitation is that participants revised notes on their own. Previous research found that revision
improved note-taking quantity most when participants revised notes during lecture pauses
with a partner (Luo et al., 2016). However, that research did not account for the considerable
number of partial ideas and images contained in students’ lesson notes. Future research could
determine if revising with a partner particularly helps students recall and finish more of these
partial ideas and images. A final limitation pertains to the ecological validity of the research
procedures. Participants were not provided access to the PowerPoint slides used in the video
lecture while they took notes, although some instructors do make these materials available to
their students (Witherby & Tauber, 2019). Making such slides available could affect students’
note-taking strategies (e.g., the need to record images into their own notes). Furthermore, students might record lecture notes on devices not used in the present study, such as a mobile
phone, stylus, or tablet. Although present findings demonstrated how note-taking medium
affects recording of ideas and images, the present study procedures could not align with how
all instructors deliver lessons or how all students take notes.

Conclusion
Recent research investigating the note-taking and achievement outcomes for longhand versus
computer note taking has yielded mixed findings as shown in Table 1. Such inconsistencies in
the literature led Morehead and associates (2019a) to conclude that it was too early to declare
one note-taking medium superior to the other. The present study sought to add clarity to the
debate over the longhand and computer note-taking mediums by embedding note revision
pauses into the lesson to determine the main and interactive effects of note-taking medium
(computer versus longhand note taking) and note revision (revision versus no revision) on note
taking (complete and partial ideas and images) and achievement. Foremost, present findings
contribute to the mounting evidence (e.g., Flanigan & Titsworth, 2020; Luo et al., 2018; Mueller & Oppenheimer, 2014) that computer note taking does not consistently lead to the learning benefits that many college students surmise. When revision pauses are embedded within
ongoing lectures, present findings suggest that writing notes longhand is more advantageous
for note completeness and achievement than typing notes on a computer. Thus, this round in
the computer versus longhand note-taking debate goes to the combination of longhand note
taking and revision, which resulted in more notes and higher achievement than computer note
taking with revision.
