Abstract
                                            pe


   Recent data support the idea that movements play a crucial role in letter representation and sug-
gest that handwriting knowledge contributes to visual recognition of letters. If so, using diﬀerent
motor activities while subjects are learning to write should aﬀect their subsequent recognition perfor-
mances. In order to test this hypothesis, we trained adult participants to write new characters either by
copying them or by typing them on a keyboard. After three weeks of training we ran a series of tests
                          's




requiring visual processing of the characters’ orientation. Tests were ran immediately, one week after,
and three weeks after the end of the training period. Results showed that when the characters had
been learned by typing, they were more frequently confused with their mirror images than when they
                        or




had been written by hand. This handwriting advantage did not appear immediately, but mostly three
weeks after the end of the training. Our results therefore suggest that the stability of the characters’
representation in memory depends on the nature of the motor activity produced during learning.

1. Introduction

    Nowadays, most adults write using a keyboard and computers are being increasingly
used at school, even at the kindergarten stage, by very young children. This mutation
of the writing habits raises general questions about the possible contribution of writing
movements to the development of written language representations. If one happens to
learn how to write directly with a keyboard, will those representations be aﬀected? In fact,
some studies indicate that motor–perceptual links contribute to the representation of the




                                                                            y
very basic units of written language: alphabetic characters, which are usually associated




                                                                    p
with highly speciﬁc writing movements. In pathology, it has been shown for instance that
a brain lesion provoking the inability to write (motor agraphia) can also induce deﬁcits in




                                                                 co
visually identifying letters (Anderson, Damasio, & Damasio, 1990). Conversely, writing
movements can help patients whose reading abilities are impaired: clinical reports showed
that patients with pure alexia, who were no longer able to recognize letters, sometimes suc-
ceeded when they were asked to trace the outline of the letters with their ﬁngers (Bartol-
omeo, Bachoud-Lévi, Chokron, & Degos, 2002; Déjerine, 1892; Seki, Yajima, & Sugishita,



                                                       al
1995). These studies suggest that visual and sensorimotor representations of letter shapes
are tightly coupled. Handwriting movements may therefore be activated in memory by the
visual presentation of letters. Longcamp, Anton, Roth, and Velay (2003) investigated this
                                               on
question in a functional MRI study and reported that in right-handed participants, the
simple visual presentation of isolated letters activated a premotor zone in the left hemi-
sphere although no motor response was required. The same zone was strongly activated
                                          rs

when the participants were actually writing the letters. Furthermore, the symmetrical area
in the right hemisphere was activated when left-handed participants were passively watch-
ing letters, conﬁrming the dependence of this visually induced activation on the writing
                                   pe


hand (Longcamp, Anton, Roth, & Velay, 2005). These various data indicate that the cere-
bral representation of letters might not be strictly visual, but might be based on a complex
neural network including a sensorimotor component acquired while learning concomi-
tantly to read and write. If so, changing the motor conditions during learning by using typ-
ing instead of handwriting will probably aﬀect the participants’ representation of the
                     's




letters and hence their subsequent recognition performances. Longcamp, Zerbato-Pou-
dou, and Velay (2005b) have studied two groups of preschool children who were learning
                   or




letters either by handwriting or by typing, and compared letter recognition performances
following training. Results showed that in the older children of the sample, handwriting
training gave rise to higher letter recognition performances than typing training.
              th




    Identifying letters requires the ability to recognize their orientation. Indeed, orientation
is a critical factor when readers have to discriminate between letters, since ‘d’ diﬀers in its
orientation from ‘b’, as does ‘p’ from ‘q’. In fact, confusions between letters and their mir-
      Au




ror images are among the most frequent errors made by young children and ‘poor readers’
(Adams, 1990; Terepocki, Kruk, & Willows, 2002). Writing movements may contribute to
memorizing the orientation of characters. The aim of the present experiment was to inves-
tigate this question, by teaching adult participants new characters, either by copying them
with a pen or by typing them on a predesigned keyboard. Following this training, we
tested the performance in explicitly recognizing the orientation of the characters (mir-
ror–normal judgment tests). In addition, we also ran control tests requiring visual discrim-
ination of the characters, but no explicit recognition (same–diﬀerent tests) in order to
question the possible eﬀect of the learning modalities at more basic levels of processing.
648                 M. Longcamp et al. / Human Movement Science 25 (2006) 646–656

Please note that we will refer to ‘writing’ (or ‘handwriting’) and ‘typing’ to designate the
two learning modalities. However those terms should be understood in their basic form,
i.e., as motor activities, since the characters learned did not have any phonological or lin-
guistic valence.

2. Methods

2.1. Participants




                                                                             p      y
   Twelve participants, 7 males and 5 females, with a mean age of 25 years, participated in
the experiment. All were right-handed. Manual laterality was assessed using the Edin-




                                                                          co
burgh Handedness Inventory (Oldﬁeld, 1971).

2.2. Procedure

   Two distinct sets of 10 unknown characters (modiﬁed from the Bengali and Gujarati



                                                              al
alphabets) were learned (Fig. 1). Six of the participants learned the ﬁrst set by typing it
and the other set by writing it and the reverse for the six other participants. The experi-
ment was run in two phases: ﬁrst, a ‘learning’ phase and then a ‘testing’ phase.
                                                      on
2.2.1. Learning
   The learning period lasted for three weeks, and consisted of one hour session per week.
                                               rs

The two learning modes were ran sequentially in the same session, in an order counterbal-
anced between participants and between sessions. The visual presentation of the characters
was rigorously identical in both learning methods. The participants were seated, facing a
                                       pe


computer monitor on which 3 cm high characters were displayed for 5 s. Each character
of both sets was written (or typed) 20 times a session. For each set, the 200 presentations were
divided in 4 blocks of 50 with 5 min rest between. The characters were presented in a random
order.
                        's




2.2.1.1. Typing training. Participants were required to ﬁnd the appropriate character on the
keyboard, and to type it. No instruction was given relative to the ﬁnger to be used, and
                      or




most participants spontaneously used their index ﬁngers to type the characters. No con-
straints were imposed regarding the speed but participants had to type the character within
the 5 s it was presented. After the 5 s, a sound signalled the beginning of a new trial. Two
                 th




keyboards had been previously adapted for the purpose of the study: all the keys except 10
were removed. The 10 remaining keys were in the central part of the keyboard, and the
new characters were designed on the keys by means of adhesive labels. In function of
       Au




the set they had to learn, participants used one or the other keyboard.




                         Fig. 1. The two sets of 10 characters used for learning.
                  M. Longcamp et al. / Human Movement Science 25 (2006) 646–656             649

2.2.1.2. Handwriting training. Participants were required to write the characters on a paper
sheet, which was ﬁxed on a tablet. Four sheets on which 50 squares were designed were
prepared in advance. The paper was changed between each block. No constraints were
imposed regarding the speed but participants had to write the character within the 5 s it
was presented. After the 5 s, a sound signalled the beginning of a new trial. If the character
was not completely written when the following was displayed, participants had to stop
writing it and to look at the monitor for the following character.




                                                                            y
2.2.2. Tests




                                                                    p
   During the tests, participants were seated at a distance of 57 cm from the computer
monitor with their head in a chin and forehead restraint and their eyes at the level of




                                                                 co
the screen center. Stimuli were displayed for 100 ms. Responses were recorded on the
stimulus-control computer. If no response was made within 2 s, the trial was coded as
an error.

2.2.2.1. Post-learning recognition tests. The recognition tests were based on a mirror–



                                                       al
normal judgment task. The characters were presented either in normal or in mirror-
reversed form and participants were instructed to respond as quickly and precisely as
possible whether the character displayed was normally oriented (right-hand press) or
                                               on
mirror-reversed (left-hand press). To perform the task, participants had to explicitly recall
the shape of the characters from memory. The characters were displayed at the center of
the screen. They subtended a vertical visual angle of 3°.
                                          rs

   Overall, there were 40 characters (20 normal and 20 mirrored), which were presented 3
times each, in a random order, for a total of 120 trials. A ﬁrst test was run immediately at
the end of the last learning session (W0) and repeated twice: one week (W1) and 3 weeks
                                   pe


(W3) later. Before the ﬁrst test, 64 trials were displayed with digits to make the partici-
pants familiar with the task requirements.

2.2.2.2. Discrimination tests. The discrimination tests were based on a ‘same–diﬀerent’ task
(Posner & Mitchell, 1967). To perform this task, participants could rely on a visual analysis
                     's




of the display that did not require explicit recall and thus any previous knowledge of the
characters. This feature allowed us to check the discrimination performance before learning
                   or




the two sets of characters and, in addition to these two sets, to use a supplementary set of 10
control characters (complexity matched with the learned characters). Such a control condi-
tion could not be implemented in the recognition test. Two images of the same character
              th




were presented simultaneously on each side of the central ﬁxation cross, and the partici-
pants were instructed to respond as quickly and precisely as possible whether the two char-
acters were identically oriented or not. The two characters were either both normally
      Au




oriented (response ‘yes’), or both mirror-oriented (response ‘yes’), or one normal- and
one mirror-oriented (response ‘no’). One hundred and twenty trials were run per test (half
with response ‘yes’ and half with response ‘no’). The eccentricity of the characters relative
to the central cross was calculated so that the display could be perceived easily in central
vision. Discrimination performances were tested four times: – before the beginning of the
training (pretest), – immediately after the last learning session (W0), – one week later
(W1), – 3 weeks later (W3). Unfortunately, data from 6 out of the 12 participants could
not be recorded at W3, due to a technical problem. We will therefore present data for
the 12 participants at pretest, W0 and W1, and data for only 6 participants at W3.
650                    M. Longcamp et al. / Human Movement Science 25 (2006) 646–656

   Before the ﬁrst test, 64 trials were displayed with digits to make the participants familiar
with the task requirements.

4. Discussion
                  th




   The main result of this study was that learning the characters by handwriting facilitated
their subsequent recognition. This result is in agreement with previous investigations in chil-
         Au




dren (Hulme, 1979; Longcamp, Zerbato-Poudou, et al., 2005b), and in Chinese adult partic-
ipants reading ideograms (Flores d’Arcais, 1994). It brings further information about the
functional signiﬁcance of such a motor contribution, by showing that handwriting knowl-
edge may in particular help the participants to orient the characters correctly in space.

4.1. Handwriting vs. typing

  From the sensori-motor point of view, handwriting and typing are clearly distinct and
probably rely on diﬀerent central processes even if, in both cases, a hand movement is
                     M. Longcamp et al. / Human Movement Science 25 (2006) 646–656                         653




                                                                             p        y
                                                                          co
                                                               al
                                                      on
Fig. 5. Mean reaction times values for control, typed and handwritten characters, for the ﬁrst 3 discrimination
                                                rs

tests (12 participants (a)) and for the last discrimination test (6 participants (b)).
                                        pe


associated with the visual image of a given character. On the one hand, handwriting
requires the participant to produce a sequence of movements that completely deﬁnes
the shape of the character in order to build an internal model of it. Once learning is com-
pleted, a unique correspondence is created between a character and the corresponding
movement. On the other hand, typing required the participants to learn the association
                        's




between an arbitrary cue (the character, deﬁned by its position in space) and a simple
motor response, and to build up a cognitive map of the keyboard (see for instance, Logan,
                      or




1999). But since the trajectory of the ﬁnger to a given key depends on its position before it
starts moving, no speciﬁc relationship between the visual form of a character and a given
movement can be formed. Moreover, there is nothing in this pointing movement that
                th




informs about the orientation of the characters. In brief, handwriting provides on-line sig-
nals from several sources including vision, motor commands and kinaesthetic feedback,
which are closely linked and simultaneously distributed in time. No such spatio-temporal
       Au




pattern occurs in typewriting.
   Due to those diﬀerences, handwriting is associated with more consistent spatial infor-
mation about a character. The order of the strokes, the direction of the horizontal seg-
ments and the rotational direction of the movement (clockwise or counter-clockwise)
are memorized for a given character (Goodnow & Levine, 1973; Meulenbroek &
Thomassen, 1991). In the present recognition task, the detection of a match or a mismatch
between the perceived shape and the memorized motor program might contribute to
the mirror–normal recognition processes and therefore explain the behavioral facilita-
tion for the characters learned by handwriting. This explanation is strengthened by the
654               M. Longcamp et al. / Human Movement Science 25 (2006) 646–656

assumption that such a task relies on a mental rotation (Hamm, Johnson, & Corballis,
2004). Interestingly, several recent studies have already demonstrated that mental rotation
of 3D meaningless objects is not a purely visual task, but involves a motor component
(mental simulation of the manual rotation of the objects, Richter et al., 2000; Wexler,
Kosslyn, & Berthoz, 1998). More generally, motor knowledge is considered important
to process spatial information from vision (Paillard, 1971, 1991). Our results therefore
extend this perspective to written language by showing that mental rotation of characters
is more eﬃcient when they are associated with handwriting memory.




                                                                              y
   Finally, it could be objected that the observed eﬀects are due to diﬀerences in the com-




                                                                      p
plexity of the tasks performed during training, rather than in the nature of those tasks. The
copying task might have required more attention and more time, resulting in more stable




                                                                   co
representations of the characters. Even if the time during which characters were displayed
was rigorously the same in copying and typing, this objection cannot be totally ruled out
because within the 5 s presentation the time spent to write a character was probably longer
than the time spent to type it. However, those attentional diﬀerences are hardly distin-
guishable from the underlying motor activity. Furthermore, in typing, participants had



                                                        al
more time to observe the characters and thus, to memorize their correct visual shape
and orientation.
                                                 on
4.2. Retention over short vs. longer time intervals

   We observed that performances following handwriting and typing training did not dif-
                                           rs

fer signiﬁcantly when the test occurred immediately after the learning phase, but that the
advantage for handwriting emerged 1 week, and mostly 3 weeks later. Recent ﬁndings
about motor memory help to explain this delayed advantage. With suﬃcient practice,
                                      pe


the internal model of a new motor skill gradually (within a few hours) becomes less fragile
and this consolidation is accompanied by changes in the neural representation of motor
memory (Penhune & Doyon, 2002; Shadmehr & Holcomb, 1997). Once it has been thor-
oughly learned and stabilized, motor memory can last for very long periods of time with-
out any further practice (Shadmehr & Brashers-Krug, 1997), and some studies even show
                      's




an improvement of performances after consolidation (Brashers-Krug, Shadmehr, & Bizzi,
1996). In the ﬁrst test, which was run a few minutes after the third training session, the
                    or




motor memory was presumably not yet consolidated, and recognition relied mainly on
the visual memory of the images of the characters. After some time, the motor memory
stabilized and the internal model might have helped, in addition to visual memory, to dif-
               th




ferentiate between characters and their mirror images. The contribution of this motor
memory can occur only if a motor program has actually been built up during practice,
and this does not seem likely to occur in the case of typing. That might explain why the
       Au




performances are stable for the handwritten characters whereas they decrease with time
for the printed characters (following general rules of forgetting) (Wixted & Ebbesen,
1991).

4.3. Discrimination vs. recognition

   In the same–diﬀerent task (discrimination tests) participants could rely on a simple
perceptual analysis of the display, whereas they had to explicitly recall the shape of the
character in memory to be able to judge their orientation (recognition tests). The discrimi-
                       M. Longcamp et al. / Human Movement Science 25 (2006) 646–656                              655

nation task can be achieved with unknown stimuli (the control characters in the pre-test)
and the performance did not improve when the characters were learned (the increase in
discrimination performances after training is probably explained by a habituation to the
task, since the eﬀect was the same for the control characters which had not been learned).
The dissociation between a clear modality eﬀect in the recognition task and the lack of
eﬀect in the discrimination task thus remains to be explained.
   A ﬁrst tentative explanation for this dissociation involves task features: motor knowl-
edge would get involved in explicit memory of the character’s orientation (probed in the




                                                                                           y
recognition task), but not in implicit memory (probed in the discrimination task). Since




                                                                                  p
implicit memory is thought to be more sensitive to contextual changes between learning
and test (hyperspeciﬁcity, Jacoby & Dallas, 1981; Srinivas, 1993) it is possible that the dis-




                                                                               co
crimination task cannot beneﬁt from the transfer of the writing knowledge, contrarily to
explicit memory. A second possible explanation, that we favor, involves task complexity
rather than task features. The writing knowledge associated with the shape of the charac-
ter would be functionally pertinent at higher levels of processing required in the recogni-
tion test, but not when the task relies on lower levels of immediate visual processing such



                                                                   al
as in the discrimination test. Irrespective of the task, when visual processing by itself is not
suﬃcient to bring decisive information, a higher weight could be put on the motor coding
acquired during learning. This explanation is in line with clinical observations of patients
                                                          on
suﬀering damage in the brain regions involved in the visual processing of letters and
objects, who spontaneously use motor strategies to solve recognition tasks (see Bartolo-
meo et al., 2002; Seki et al., 1995; Sirigu, Duhamel, & Poncet, 1991).
                                                   rs

5. Conclusion
                                           pe


   Our results indicate that the writing movements produced during learning aﬀected the
participants’ ability to subsequently diﬀerentiate the characters from their mirror images.
It can be assumed that handwriting movements contribute to characters representation
and to visual recognition processes.
